Preferential interaction coefficients, re-read at 10 ns

Same 12 conditions, same two questions as the first pass — but the trajectories were re-run at 10 ns instead of 1 ns, and the numbers changed enough to redo the whole analysis. This revision replaces the earlier read; the 1 ns figures are kept below only where they explain what moved.

System AAV1 capsid exterior, component 3 = sucrose Metric Γ₂₃(r*), cumulative preferential interaction coefficient Batch pic_suc_7_9 / 10ns-simulations

What changed

The short answer: the 1 ns runs weren't equilibrated, and it showed.

1 ns → 10 ns

Same formulation (pH 6 / 30 mM / 12% sucrose, condition D): at 1 ns, Γ₂₃ climbed to −347 by r*=30 Å and never stopped climbing. At 10 ns, the curve now does something physically sensible — a sharp excluded-volume dip right at the surface, a rise back through zero around r≈5 Å, then a shallow, still-gently-declining tail out to −44 by r*=15 Å. Magnitudes across the board dropped 5−10×, one condition (pH 6 / 30 mM / 1% sucrose) flipped sign entirely (−51 → +3.2), and per-condition SEMs went up, not down — consistent with the 1 ns trajectories being kinetically stuck near their starting configuration rather than genuinely low-noise.

Practical upshot: treat everything from the first pass as superseded. The formulation-ranking correlation is now much cleaner, the DOE story is different (pH×sucrose interaction, not a sucrose-dominant main effect), and the 3-fold/5-fold symmetry finding from before does not hold up — see §3.

This is now about as clean a match as four points can give: Γ₂₃ orders the formulations exactly as C ≈ F < D << E, matching the known ranking rank-for-rank, with E standing out as a large, unambiguous outlier (15.6× its combined SEM away from D) rather than a marginal call. 2 · DOE sweep (pH 6–8, 30–200 mM NaCl, 1–10% sucrose)

All 8 corners, r*=15 Å.

  • The dominant story is now a pH×sucrose interaction, not a sucrose main effect. A plain main-effects fit (pH + salt + sucrose, no interaction) only reaches R²≈0.78 with residuals up to ±24 — clearly missing something. Adding a single pH×sucrose term brings it to R²≈0.99. Concretely: going from 1%→10% sucrose costs about −15 to −21 at pH 6, but −80 to −96 at pH 8 — roughly a 5× larger sucrose penalty at the higher pH. pH on its own, independent of sucrose level, has almost no effect (main-effect coefficient collapses to ≈−1.5/unit once the interaction is in the model).
  • Salt is still negligible — coefficient ≈+0.02/mM either way, and its sign isn't consistent across pH/sucrose combinations (e.g. at pH 8/10% sucrose, 30→200 mM NaCl actually pulls Γ₂₃ less negative, −107→−92). Not a real driver at this radius.
  • This reframes the formulation-ranking result: D and E share identical salt/sucrose (30 mM, 12%) and differ only in pH (6 vs. 7.3) — exactly the pH×sucrose interaction regime where this DOE fit predicts the biggest pH sensitivity, since 12% sucrose is the highest level tested. That's consistent with D and E being the two formulations that pulled apart the most in §1.

3 · Three-fold vs. five-fold axis regions — reversed, not confirmed

All 12 conditions now have pic_symmetry_regions output (the pH 7.3 chain-count bug from the last pass is fixed and no longer blocks this).

Correction

The earlier pass (1 ns, r*=5 Å) found a consistent, sucrose-gated pattern: the 5-fold pore more negative than the 3-fold protrusions once sucrose exceeded ~8%. At 10 ns that pattern does not reproduce — in most conditions the direction has flipped (5-fold now less negative than 3-fold), and the handful of cases that still favor a 5-fold-more-negative reading are the smallest, least significant ones. This was exactly the kind of structural finding that under-sampling can manufacture: with the underlying Γ₂₃ landscape still evolving at 1 ns, a small, consistent-looking regional bias doesn't mean much.

  • Direction now mostly reads the other way — 5-fold less negative than 3-fold in 10 of 12 conditions (only pH 8/200 mM/1% sucrose still shows 5-fold more negative, and it's a small gap). The strongest, clearest gaps are formulation C (+0.263, ~19× combined SEM) and the two pH 6/10%-sucrose DOE corners (~7–9× SEM) — those are real, just opposite in sign from what the 1 ns data suggested.
  • Several points are within noise — D (+0.013, <1× SEM), pH 8/200mM/10% (+0.005, <1× SEM), and a couple of the 1%-sucrose points sit at or below their combined SEM. This isn't a clean monotonic-with-sucrose story the way the 1 ns data appeared to be.
  • Net read: there may be a real 3-fold/5-fold structural difference here, but the direction and the "gated by sucrose%" story from the first pass shouldn't be trusted, and the current picture is noisy enough that I wouldn't lean on it either way without another independent run (a true replicate, not just longer sampling of the same trajectory) to see which direction is reproducible.

4 · Convergence, revisited

Better, not solved

10 ns fixed the worst of it — curves now show a physically sensible shape instead of monotonically ballooning — but r*=15 Å still isn't a clean plateau for every condition. E in particular (Fig. 1, bottom curve) is still declining at a visible slope right at r*=15 Å, meaning its true asymptotic value may be even more negative than −135. pic.sh's own comments note 15−20 Å is the typical target and to "always check the saved plot to confirm the plateau was reached" — worth extending E (and probably D) out to 20 Å to check before leaning too hard on the exact magnitude of the C/F/D/E gaps, even though the ranking itself already looks robust at 15 Å.

Loose ends

  • E's curve (and to a lesser extent D's) hadn't clearly flattened by r*=15 Å — worth a longer radius if the exact magnitude (not just the ranking) matters downstream.
  • The 3-fold/5-fold split is now an open question rather than a finding in either direction — needs an independent replicate before it's worth acting on.
  • 4-point ranking correlation is still only 4 points. The pattern is much stronger than before, but C vs. D is still a soft call (~1.9× SEM) worth keeping an eye on as more formulations get ranked.
  • Given how much the 1 ns → 10 ns jump changed the picture, it's worth asking whether 10 ns itself is long enough, given §4 — a spot-check at 20 ns on just the outlier conditions (E, D) would be the cheapest way to find out.
